What Happens When You Make an AI the Dungeon Master

2026-03-244 min read (759 words)
#ai  #dnd  #automation  #openclaw 

I run OpenClaw — a self-hosted AI agent that has access to my email, my calendar, my shell, and apparently now my Dungeons & Dragons campaign.

Yes. I made an AI the Dungeon Master for Curse of Strahd. Here’s what happened.

Why#

Curse of Strahd is one of the best-written D&D modules ever made. It’s a gothic horror sandbox set in a fog-drenched land ruled by a vampire count who is simultaneously the most compelling villain and the most insufferable ex in tabletop history. It deserves a DM who can hold the tone.

My group kept losing DMs to real life — jobs, moves, kids, the general entropy of adulthood. We kept getting three sessions in and stalling. I decided to stop waiting for a human who had the time and the commitment, and started building one that didn’t need either.

The Setup#

The AI DM runs through OpenClaw with a dedicated Discord bot and its own set of channels: #motd for setting the scene, #gameplay for live sessions, #characters for the party roster, #above-table for out-of-character logistics.

The agent has access to the full Curse of Strahd source material in its context. It knows the NPCs, the locations, the random encounter tables, Strahd’s motivations, the Death House, all of it. It maintains a persistent memory of what the party has done, who they’ve killed, what alliances they’ve made, and which taverns they’ve burned down.

Players interact through Discord. The DM responds. Sessions can happen asynchronously — someone posts an action at 11pm, the DM responds, someone else picks it up at 7am. The fog of Barovia doesn’t care about your work schedule.

Where It Actually Works#

Atmosphere. This is where it genuinely surprised me. Gothic horror lives and dies by the prose, and the AI writes it well. The descriptions of Castle Ravenloft, the Amber Temple, the dead village of Barovia — they land. There’s a particular kind of melancholy the model reaches for naturally that fits Strahd like a coffin lid.

NPC consistency. Strahd is hard to play because he’s not just a villain — he’s a tragic figure who believes himself the protagonist of his own story. The AI maintains that characterization across sessions with a consistency that most human DMs struggle with. Every letter from Strahd to the party feels like it was written by the same obsessed, eloquent monster.

Rules adjudication. The model knows the 5e ruleset well enough to handle most combat and skill check situations without breaking the flow. It’s not perfect, but it’s consistent, which matters more in practice.

Where It Breaks#

Player agency. The hardest problem in D&D is also the hardest problem for an AI: improvising meaningfully when players do something completely unexpected. The model handles most lateral thinking, but there’s a ceiling. When players get genuinely creative — or genuinely chaotic — the DM sometimes retreats to safe, predictable responses. A human DM would lean into the chaos. The AI sometimes papers over it.

Long-term consequence tracking. Sessions accumulate. An NPC the party offended four sessions ago should remember. A door they broke should still be broken. The AI’s memory is good but not perfect, and Curse of Strahd is dense. Periodic memory summaries help, but it’s still a weak point.

Comedy. Gothic horror can coexist with dark comedy — that’s half of what makes Strahd work. The AI plays the dramatic beats well but sometimes misses the timing on the funny ones. A skilled human DM knows when to let the moment land. The AI occasionally over-explains the joke.

What I Actually Learned#

Making an AI the Dungeon Master is less about replacing a human and more about building a system that can hold a narrative open for people with limited time. It’s a different use case than “AI plays DM better than humans.” It’s “AI keeps the game alive when humans can’t show up consistently.”

For that specific problem, it works remarkably well.

The deeper lesson: the same properties that make AI assistants useful for security work — consistent application of rules, persistent memory, availability at any hour — make them surprisingly good at running tabletop games. The underlying capability is the same. You’re just pointed at a different problem.

Barovia will still be there when you get back. Strahd is patient. He’s been waiting 400 years. Your schedule doesn’t impress him.

Built with OpenClaw. The AI DM repo is at rbfp/openclaw-ai-dungeon-master — private for now, but documented if you want to build something similar.
